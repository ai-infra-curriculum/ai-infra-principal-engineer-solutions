# SOLUTION — Mentorship and Technical Leadership

> Read this *after* you have attempted the deliverables. These
> "solutions" are rubrics for behavior, not code. This document
> explains *why* technical mentorship at the principal level is
> shaped the way it is — and how it differs from management.

## What this module is really teaching

Mentorship at the principal-engineer level is *not*:

- Management (you're not their manager).
- Tutoring (they're not your students).
- Sponsorship (though it often overlaps).
- Code review (though that's a tool).

It *is*: the deliberate transfer of **judgment** that takes
years to develop — judgment about what's worth working on, what
quality bar to hold, how to think about ambiguity, when to push
back on leadership, when to compromise on craft. Principal
engineers are the people other engineers' careers look most
like if things go well.

The thing the module is really teaching: at this altitude, your
output is no longer measured by what *you* ship. It's measured
by what the engineers you've shaped ship over the next 5-10
years. Acting on that is uncomfortable for most former
ship-it-yourself ICs.

## What the deliverables should actually look like

### Case study (exercise 01): a mentor relationship that mattered

Pick a mentor relationship — yours with someone, or two famous
ones (e.g., Bill Joy mentoring at Sun, Jeff Dean's early
mentorship of Google engineers). Then dissect:

- What specifically did the mentor *do* that mattered? Not
  "encouraged me" — "introduced me to X, pushed back on Y,
  said Z at exactly the right moment."
- What didn't they do? Where did they let the mentee struggle
  productively?
- What's the **transferable pattern**?

Good case studies focus on **specific moments**. A relationship
that was "generally good" produces no insights. A relationship
where the mentor's intervention on a specific code review or
project changed the trajectory teaches something.

Common failure modes:
- **Hagiography**: the mentor was wonderful and the mentee
  flourished. No mechanism, no lesson.
- **"They believed in me"**: probably true; not actionable.
- **Confusing mentor with manager**: management and mentorship
  are different relationships; conflating them loses the
  distinction.

### Strategy memo (exercise 02): the mentorship plan

A principal-engineer's mentorship "strategy" is not a HR
program. It's a personal plan that addresses:

1. **Who am I currently mentoring?** Name them. Be specific.
2. **What do they need from me, that they're unlikely to get
   elsewhere?** (Honest answer: usually 2-3 things per person.)
3. **What am I spending on this?** Time per week, per person.
4. **What's the right ceiling?** Most principals can sustain 3-5
   active mentees with depth. Past that, you're being a hallway
   acquaintance, not a mentor.
5. **Who am I *not* mentoring deliberately?** Saying yes to
   everyone makes you useless to everyone.

A useful memo also covers **the org-wide layer**: how does the
principal-engineer community at this company develop the next
generation? What rituals exist? Which need to be created?

Common failure modes:
- **Saying yes to every mentorship ask**: dilution.
- **Mentoring only people who look like you**: a real risk and
  a real failure mode in the industry. The mentee selection
  should be intentional and inclusive.
- **No exit criteria**: a mentorship that goes on indefinitely
  is sometimes the right shape, but usually means neither side
  is moving the relationship forward.

### Stakeholder mapping (exercise 03): the development ecosystem

The "stakeholders" for technical leadership are the people who
shape the engineering culture:

- **Other principals and staff engineers** — your peers; the
  culture you're shaping is shared.
- **Engineering managers** — they need to know what you're
  doing with their reports, and they need to know they're not
  being undermined.
- **The engineers you mentor** — they have their own agency
  and goals.
- **Promotion committees** — your mentee promotion outcomes
  are partly a function of how this group reads your support.

The mapping forces you to surface the **manager
relationship**. A principal engineer who mentors someone
without the manager's awareness is at best inefficient and at
worst building friction. Bring the manager in.

### Roadmap (exercise 04): the mentee's plan

A mentorship roadmap is **the mentee's roadmap**, with the
mentor's role layered on top:

- **What's the mentee growing into?** (Specific role / scope.)
- **What's the timeline?** (Realistic — 12-24 months for a
  level transition.)
- **What are the milestones?** (Concrete projects, scopes,
  artifacts.)
- **Where does the mentor add value?** (Specific moments — the
  next big project decision, the design review, the
  promotion-packet writing.)

This is **not** a development plan that the manager writes. It's
the technical-judgment layer on top of the manager's
development plan. The two should be consistent.

Common failure modes:
- **A development plan written for the mentee instead of with
  them**: misses the point. Mentorship is a partnership.
- **No "what does the next level look like for *this* person"
  conversation**: generic level descriptions don't transfer.

### Presentation (exercise 05): leading by influence

A principal-engineer presentation as part of mentorship is
often **the mentee giving the talk while you support from
behind**. The exercise teaches:

- How to **prepare** a mentee for a technical talk so they
  succeed visibly.
- How to **bracket** their performance — be in the room, ready
  to interject if the audience derails, otherwise quiet.
- How to **handle the questions you'd answer faster** — let the
  mentee answer first, even if it's slower.

This is hard for former ICs because the impulse to answer the
technical question yourself is strong. The exercise is
deliberate practice in *not doing that*.

## Trade-offs we deliberately accepted

### Mentorship over sponsorship

Sponsorship (advocating for promotion, opening doors) is a
related but separate skill. The module focuses on mentorship
because the rubric is sharper; sponsorship rubrics tend to be
mush. Both matter.

### Limited scale

The exercises assume 3-5 active mentees per principal. There
are programs that try to scale mentorship via training, group
sessions, etc. — those are useful but they're not the
same thing as the deep individual relationships the module
focuses on.

### Western corporate norms

The frequency, format, and feedback culture of the rubrics fit
mainly US/UK/Northern European corporate norms. Adapt to local
culture; the principle (deliberate transfer of judgment) is
universal.

## Common mistakes graders see

1. **Mentoring as "answering questions"**: the mentor becomes a
   technical hotline. Useful, not mentorship.
2. **Withholding feedback because the mentee is "doing fine"**:
   the cost of withheld feedback compounds over years. The bar
   is "would I tell them this if I were their manager?" If yes,
   say it.
3. **Mentoring only the easy ones**: the high-potential, easy-to-
   relate-to engineer gets all the mentorship; the others
   don't. Real mentorship requires meeting people where they
   are.
4. **No feedback to the mentor**: mentees give feedback to
   their mentors rarely. Set up the conditions where they can.
5. **Conflating "I'm friends with them" with "I mentor them"**:
   friendship is welcome; mentorship is more structured.
6. **No succession**: a principal who mentors but never
   develops *other* mentors fails to scale.

## When to go beyond this module

- Take on a **mentee in a different specialty** than your
  primary one. The relationship forces you to articulate your
  judgment in transferable terms.
- Co-mentor with a principal architect. The two altitudes
  see different things; the mentee gets both.
- Build a **principal-engineer community** at your org —
  monthly sessions, paper readings, war-story exchanges. The
  community is the multiplier on individual mentorship.
